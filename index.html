How Canadian Shopping Habits Changed After COVID-19

Data Storytelling
by Sara Amouzesh

Shopping Online or In-Store

During the pandemic, online shopping became a lifesaver. Locked down at home, many Canadians turned to their phones and computers to buy what they needed. From early 2020 to mid-2022, online sales shot up by almost 68%! Experts even say online shopping could grow to $119 billion by 2025. But now that life feels more normal, people are heading back to stores. Younger Canadians, especially those aged 18-34, are surprising everyone. They were once all about online shopping, but now they’re starting to enjoy in-store shopping again. It’s like they’re finding a balance between the two.

Where Canadians Spend Their Money

While people are spending more money these days, they’re still not back to their pre-pandemic habits. Some categories, like cross-border shopping, are still behind where they used to be. On the other hand, canadians are also returning to restaurants while still loving delivery apps like Uber Eats and Skip the Dishes.

How Rising Costs Affect Shopping

The rising cost of living continues to change how Canadians shop. Grocery bills have gone up by 4% in the past two years, but not everyone is affected equally. Lower-income households have seen their grocery spending rise by 7%, while wealthier families experienced only a 1% increase. This shows how inflation impacts lower-income families more, making it harder for them to manage their budgets. Many Canadians are turning to budget-friendly options and cutting back on non-essential items.

Conclusion

The way Canadians shop has changed dramatically in just a few years, influenced by global events and economic challenges. Online shopping remains a big trend, but in-store visits are making a comeback. Spending habits are shifting, with some categories like dining out and beauty products on the rise, while others, like travel and children’s clothing, are slower to recover. At the same time, rising costs are forcing many Canadians to think carefully about their budgets, especially those in lower-income households. The future of shopping in Canada will likely continue to evolve, but one thing is clear: Canadians are adapting to find a balance that works for them.
